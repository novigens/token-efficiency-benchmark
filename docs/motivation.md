# Why this benchmark exists

One measurement, two reasons to take it. The business reason: model choice is a three-part decision, accuracy, dollars per correct outcome, and whether the evaluation tasks resemble your actual work, and nothing on the market prices the second part on contamination-proof tasks shaped like the third. The theoretical reason: the training recipe behind today's "reasoning models" optimizes a compromise objective that never sees cost and never assigns credit to intermediate reasoning states, and the bill for that compromise grows with task depth. This benchmark measures the bill.

## The business reason

An enterprise choosing a model for real work faces a three-part test. First, accuracy: does the configuration clear the bar the workflow demands? Second, cost: among the configurations that clear it, which delivers a correct outcome for the least money? Third, and least asked: do the tasks the model was graded on resemble the work it will actually do? Accuracy leaderboards answer the first part on their own task distribution. Price sheets answer none of it, because tokens billed per answer vary by 20x across configurations answering the same questions. The third part is where most procurement quietly goes wrong.

It goes wrong because enterprise work is, structurally, multi-step state tracking. Reconciling an invoice against a ledger, threading an order quantity from an email through a pricing rule into a fulfillment record, rolling a figure across a quarter's reports: each is a chain of small exact updates where one value must survive several representations, precisely the shape this benchmark generates fresh every run. It is not the shape of olympiad problems or public test sets, where decades of curated solutions let a model win by plan retrieval (see the theoretical section below). A model that aces pattern-dense benchmarks with a hacked version of reasoning can still drop the running count at step four of your order pipeline, and it will do so confidently. Grading models on library-free chains is the closest available proxy for that workload until the model is graded on the workload itself.

The first public run (12 configurations, identical questions, July 2026) makes the point concretely. `gpt-5.4#effort=low` reached 95% accuracy at \$0.0067 per correct answer; `claude-opus-4-8` reached the same 95% at \$0.0138 and `gpt-5.5` at \$0.0154. `moonshot:kimi-k2.6#thinking=off` reached 90% at \$0.0040; `claude-sonnet-5` reached the same 90% at \$0.0140. In every one of those pairs the premium model is dominated: same accuracy, two to seven times the cost. The only unbeaten rows on the board are a budget model with its thinking turned off (`kimi-k2.5#thinking=off`, 100% at \$0.0022) and a mid-tier model at low effort. Recency and sticker price predicted cost per outcome inversely.

Three honesty notes. First, `$/correct` is retry economics: it assumes wrong answers are detectable and re-queried. Where no oracle exists, accuracy gates everything, which is why the two numbers must be read together. Second, this board is one task class (verifiable multi-step chains), 20 tasks, single run. Whether enterprise workloads resemble this class more than they resemble pattern-lookup is the bet this benchmark makes explicit; measuring against real workloads is on the roadmap. Third, one row in our own data argues for the premium tier: `gpt-5.4#effort=medium` adds output tokens at only 32 per step of depth, so at much greater depth than we tested, rankings could shift. That is a testable claim, and the harness tests it.

The practical loop for a buyer: generate a fresh run at the depth profile of your workload, evaluate the configurations you can procure, take the cheapest row that clears your accuracy bar, and re-price the same results file whenever the price sheets change. Nothing needs re-running to re-decide.

## The theoretical reason

The training recipe behind current reasoning models is RLVR: sample several complete solutions per problem, check the final answers programmatically, and reweight the policy toward the solutions that verified. The dominant algorithm, GRPO, computes one group-normalized scalar per sampled solution and applies that same scalar as the advantage for every token in it.

Read that mechanism against what sequential decision optimization normally means. There is no per-step value function (GRPO's headline feature is deleting the critic), no temporal-difference signal, no discounting structure, and therefore no mechanism that can ever conclude "step three is where this plan went wrong." Credit is assigned to whole trajectories. Formally a token-level decision process exists; effectively the optimization is a bandit over complete plans, and recent theoretical work analyzes RLVR dynamics in exactly those terms. With binary rewards the update is close kin to rejection-sampling fine-tuning: generate candidates, keep verified winners, imitate them. The field tried per-step credit (process reward models) and largely shelved it as expensive and gameable. The formulation does not take the full benefit of RL over the problem's states; it takes a compromise that scales.

One qualification keeps this precise. A busy research literature now adds efficiency terms to RLVR, length penalties, token-significance rewards, adaptive budgets ([survey](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.10903), [length-aware RL](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.12284), [adaptive length penalties](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.05256)), and Kimi's own k1.5 paper pioneered length-penalized training. These are heuristic caps on verbosity, not the value of computation, which prices this next thought on this problem; and the shipped models we measured behave as if none of it binds: 77.8x waste on a default configuration, spend moved 8.7x by an inference-time knob at constant accuracy. The claim is therefore about deployed behavior, not about what the field knows.

The compromise has a signature, and the benchmark is built to elicit it. A stepwise reasoner's cost should track the number of state updates a problem requires. A plan-selector instead pays a fixed rumination tax while it searches its prior for a matching plan, leans on surface regularities, and at deployment has no search left when the prior misfires, because what ships is a single sample from the reshaped distribution.

Our first run shows that signature four ways:

- `kimi-k2.6` (default thinking) fits output tokens = 10,495 base minus 551 per step of depth: it spends less on deeper tasks, which is impossible for a stepwise reasoner and natural for fixed rumination dominated by the easiest prompts.
- Two models from different labs (`claude-opus-4-8`, `gpt-5.4#effort=low`) absorbed the same irrelevant "returned pallet of 63 parcels" into their totals identically, and a third subtracted it: the "every number in a word problem gets used" prior, which no state-tracker would touch.
- The same weights at different effort settings (`gpt-5.4` low vs medium) bought the same 95 to 100% accuracy band at 1.4x cost, while another vendor's knob (`kimi-k2.6` off vs on) bought 5 accuracy points at 8.7x. Spend is set by vendor policy, not by problem complexity, which is what an objective that never priced cost predicts.
- These chains are computationally easy (polynomial forward evaluation; the generator builds them by construction). The point of the benchmark is not difficulty. It is that the task distribution has no pattern library anywhere in training data, so plan retrieval has nothing to retrieve and only state-tracking produces the answer at anything near the problem's intrinsic cost, V*.

### Why depth is the discriminating axis

The two architectures diverge as chains lengthen, and they diverge in opposite currencies. For a stepwise reasoner, one more chained step is one more state update: cost grows by a small constant, and accuracy decays only with per-step reliability. For a plan-selector, every additional step and every representation joint thins the set of matching templates in its prior roughly multiplicatively; shallow word problems look like millions of training examples, but a six-step chain that crosses code, narrative, and table matches nothing. When retrieval comes up empty, the observed fallbacks are longer rumination at a fixed tax, or a confident near-miss assembled from partial patterns.

The ideal case is worth stating, because the benchmark's construction defines it exactly: accuracy flat at 100%, output growing by a few tokens per step (one state update each), cost per correct answer rising gently and linearly with the prompt, and waste constant across depth. Note the counterintuitive corollary: waste that *falls* as depth rises is not maturity but a fixed rumination tax being amortized over bigger problems.

![Cost per correct answer between depths 3 and 6, and marginal output tokens per step, with the ideal V* floor marked](../benchmark_data/runs/20260703T070656Z_098392/depth_scaling.png)

The first run already shows the split at just depths 3 and 6, and it shows the two failure modes separately. Depth-3 losses were dominated by prior misfires: the 63-parcel distractor task accounts for most of them, including two labs absorbing the same irrelevant number identically. Depth-6 losses are state-tracking decay: Sonnet 5's only two misses are confident mid-chain arithmetic slips at depth 6, and `gpt-5.4-nano` falls from 60% to 20% accuracy between the cells. On the cost side, `$/correct` roughly doubles from depth 3 to depth 6 for most rows, per-step token slopes span 32 (`gpt-5.4#effort=medium`) to 195 (`claude-sonnet-5`), and `kimi-k2.6` allocates less thinking to deeper tasks, the exact opposite of what the problems require. All of this at depths a middle schooler handles with a pencil.

That yields a falsifiable prediction, which is the benchmark's roadmap: extend the ladder to depths 10 and 14, and models that genuinely track state should show flat, small per-step cost with stable accuracy, while plan-selectors break in one of the three observed ways (compounding slips, rumination blowups, or collapse). The gentle 32-token-per-step slope of `gpt-5.4#effort=medium` makes it the candidate most likely to survive; the prediction costs a few dollars to test, and the harness's depth dial exists precisely so anyone can run it.

It also doubles as a standing challenge to any lab that disputes the diagnosis: ship a model whose cost scales like state tracking, run the replay command, and top the board. The benchmark is designed so that refuting it and vindicating it are the same experiment.

One pre-registered instance we deliberately leave to contributors: Gemini. DeepMind is the home of AlphaZero, the canonical implementation of exactly the machinery this document says is missing (state-value credit assignment, search, and compute allocated where uncertainty is highest), AlphaProof applied that recipe to formal mathematics, and DeepMind describes Deep Think's IMO gold as [inspired by AlphaGo](https://deepmind.google/blog/10-years-of-alphago/). Whether any of that machinery survives into the shipped Gemini APIs is not public. The hypothesis is cleanly falsifiable here: if the lineage is real in deployment, Gemini rows should trace the ideal signature, flat accuracy at a small per-step cost, and if they fan out like the rest of the board, the lineage stayed in the lab. The harness takes any OpenAI-compatible endpoint; this row is yours to run.

This also explains the olympiad paradox without disputing the results. Gold-medal IMO and IOI performance is real, achieved on fresh problems, in the most pattern-dense domains in existence: decades of curated solutions teach transferable plan libraries. A freshly generated warehouse ledger has no library. Different distribution, different mechanism, different bill.

The ask to the frontier labs follows directly, and it is not rhetorical: per-step credit assignment, search over the problem's semantic states rather than over sampled text, and cost inside the objective are all known techniques with literatures behind them (an active one, in the case of length-penalized RL). Choosing the compromise is a legitimate engineering decision. We claim no intent about what follows: pay-per-token billing with hidden reasoning simply means the seller's revenue rises with waste the buyer cannot inspect, a structural conflict that survives wherever waste goes unmeasured. Charging customers for the compromise's token overhead while calling it reasoning is the part this benchmark makes visible, and measurable.

## Why this is not just another leaderboard

Four properties, individually precedented, jointly not: tasks generated at runtime and replayable byte-for-byte from a seed (nothing to memorize or distill); every task anchored to its own optimal cost V*, so waste is measured against the problem rather than against other models; token counts taken from provider billing, hidden thinking included; and zero credit for wrong answers regardless of cost. The nearest neighbor, Cost-of-Pass, shares the economic quantity and runs on static public test sets. See [`related_work.md`](related_work.md) for the full map.

## Reading

- Lambert et al., Tulu 3 (2024, [arXiv:2411.15124](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.15124)): the RLVR recipe, named.
- Shao et al., DeepSeekMath (2024, [arXiv:2402.03300](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.03300)): GRPO, section 4; note the outcome-supervision advantage definition.
- DeepSeek-R1 (2025, [arXiv:2501.12948](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.12948)): reasoning emergence from pure RLVR; watch the response-length curves.
- Yue et al. (2025, [arXiv:2504.13837](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.13837)): does RLVR expand capability or sharpen sampling toward what the base model already finds?
- Lightman et al., Let's Verify Step by Step (2023): process rewards, the per-step credit road not taken.
- Mirzadeh et al., GSM-Symbolic (2024): accuracy drops under surface variation of known problems.
- [awesome-RLVR](https://github.com/opendilab/awesome-RLVR): continually updated index, including bandit-view analyses of RLVR dynamics.

For the lineage the compromise sets aside: Bellman, Dynamic Programming (1957); Minsky, Steps Toward Artificial Intelligence (1961), which named the credit assignment problem; Sutton, Learning to Predict by the Methods of Temporal Differences (1988); and Russell and Wefald, Principles of Metareasoning (1991), which formalized the value of computation, the idea that deliberation itself has a price and an optimal stopping point. The two things current RLVR implementations omit, per-step credit and a cost on thinking, are the two things this literature exists to provide; the omission is a choice of implementation, not a limitation of the paradigm.
